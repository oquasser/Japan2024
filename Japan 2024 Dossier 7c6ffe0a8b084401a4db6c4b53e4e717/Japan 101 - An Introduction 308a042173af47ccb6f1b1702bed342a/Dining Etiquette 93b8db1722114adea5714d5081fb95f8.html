Dining Etiquette

Dining rituals are fundamental in Japan. While we’re there on our trip, there will undoubtedly be occasions where we’ll be dining in “fancy” places, and knowing a little dining etiquette in those spots will go a long way - we’ll definitely stand out from typical tourists if we keep these rules in mind. In addition to the tips below, there are some standard phrases to be used before and after dining, but those are both covered later in the “Useful Japanese Phrases” section of the dossier.

At the Start of the Meal

O-shibori occurs at the start of the meal. Before touching anything, a warm towel is provided to wipe your hands. Unlike in China, this is not okay to use on your face. In homes or places without the warm towel, you are expected to wash your hands just prior.

Using Chopsticks the Right Way

Using chopsticks has its own unique etiquette, called ohashi. You can easily come off as rude or insult fellow diners (or the chef!) without following the usage rules below.

  • Be sure not to point, wave, or gesture with chopsticks.
  • Do not lick chopsticks.
  • When you are using chopsticks, be careful not to rub them together. That can indicate that you think the chopsticks are of poor quality.
  • Don’t use your chopsticks as a skewer to stab food.
  • It’s rude to talk while holding your chopsticks - put down your chopsticks while talking.
  • Break wooden chopsticks apart over your lap, not the plate.
  • If there is a serving set of chopsticks, use those and not your own to serve yourself from a shared plate. If there is no serving set of chopsticks, use the back end of your chopsticks instead.
  • Do not pass food with chopsticks, which is a common ritual at Buddhist funerals.
  • Do not pull bowls or plates with your chopsticks.
  • Avoid sticking your chopsticks straight up in your rice - this is another funeral rite.

Eating Food From a Bowl

It’s okay to slurp, especially when eating udon or soup. If eating from small bowls, it’s okay to bring the bowl to your mouth or chest rather than spill. Also, do not leave shells in the bowl they were served in. If you have a dish with shells, like clams, put the emptied shells onto a separate plate or the lid of the bowl.

Eating Sushi

  • Avoid putting a lot of soy sauce in your dipping bowl. You should only have a trace left when done.
  • Don’t put wasabi directly in the soy sauce dipping bowl.
  • It’s ok to eat nigiri with your hands, but sashimi should be eaten with chopsticks.
  • When eating nigiri, make sure to dip the fish side into the soy sauce, not the rice side.
  • Eat nigiri sushi in one bite.
  • The ginger is a palate cleanser and is meant to be eaten between bites of different types of sushi. Don’t eat it with in the same bite as your sushi.
  • If ordering omakase, the sushi chef usually prepares each bite to be eaten as he sees fit - avoid using any soy sauce on an omakase plate. Omakase essentially means “as you recommend”, and if you’re adding soy sauce to it, it’s not the way the chef has recommended it.
  • Typically, sushi should be eaten immediately after being prepared and served - ideally, it should be eaten in 10 seconds.

At the End of a Meal

After finishing your meal, it is generally good manner to return all your dishes to how they were at the start of the meal. This includes replacing the lids on dishes and putting your chopsticks back on the chopstick rest or in its paper holder.

Paying for the Meal

The bill will be presented face down either as you receive the meal or after you have finished eating. In most restaurants you are supposed to bring your bill to the cashier near the exit when leaving, as it is not common to pay at the table. Paying in cash remains common, but many restaurants also accept payment by credit cards and IC cards, such as Suica.

Some restaurants, especially cheaper ones, have slightly different systems for ordering and paying. In many ramen and gyudon restaurants, for example, "meal tickets" are bought at a vending machine near the store's entrance upon entering and handed over to the staff who then prepare and serve the meal.
