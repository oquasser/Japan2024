Kyoto Area Overview

Kyoto is roughly divided into five regions: Southern, Western, Central, Eastern, and Northern Kyoto. Most regions are further subdivided into different districts, each with their own distinct atmosphere and ambiance.

Northern Kyoto (Kitayama - 北山)

Northern Kyoto is graced with scores of centuries-old shrines and temples, including several World Heritage Sites. One of Kyoto’s most famous attractions - the magnificent golden pavilion of Kinkaku-ji - can be found here. Northern Kyoto can be further broken down into smaller areas, each offering a different experience.

Northwestern Kyoto

This is where the bulk of the temples are in this region of Kyoto, including the famous Kinkaku-ji. It is a bit of a hike to get here, so it’s probably best to tackle several of the tourist destinations located here in a single day. Northwestern Kyoto encompasses a large part of the city, north of Imadegawa-dori and west of Senbon-dori. Buses, taxis, or bicycles are the best way to explore this area.

Takao Area

While there are not a lot of tourist destinations in this part of Northern Kyoto, it is one of Kyoto’s most famous places to view the fall leaves. Throughout autumn, the place is lively with vendors selling fresh treats and lanterns along the river at night. It takes an hour to get here from the city proper by bus. For what it’s worth, the autumn colors tend to peak here around the middle of November.

Northeastern Kyoto

In the northeastern area of Kyoto, there is a wealth of temples and shrines which are less crowded than those found in the Kyoto city area. Unfortunately, the lack of crowds comes with a cost, as it takes quite a while to get to this part of Kyoto. This part of Kyoto lies in the curve of the mountain range that shelters Kyoto City.

Kurama

Kurama is a rural town in the northern mountains of Kyoto City, less than one hour from the city center. Kurama’s main attraction is the Kurama-dera, a Buddhist temple located along the steep wooded mountainside above the town. Mt. Kurama is known for being the birthplace of the holistic healing art of reiki.

Ohara Area

Ohara is another rural mountain town north of Kyoto. It takes about 40 minutes to get here by bus from Kyoto Station, and has many temples and shrines. It also tends to welcome the fall foliage a week or so earlier than the rest of Kyoto.

Western Kyoto (Arashiyama - 嵐山)

Arashima is the second-most important sightseeing district in Kyoto. It’s filled with temples and shrines, but the start attraction is the famed Arashiyama Bamboo Grove. This area is in the far west of Kyoto, tucked along the base of the Arashiyama Mountains. Regardless of how you get here (train, bus, bike, or taxi), it takes about 30 minutes from central Kyoto. The main street of Arashiyama, along with the famed Togetsu-kyo Bridge, tends to be a bit of a tourist circus. This is also a great area to check out the fall foliage, but that tends to happen in the second half of November.

Central Kyoto

This is where the ultra-modern Kyoto Station is located, and that station itself tends to draw in a lot of visitors. Central Kyoto also contains two of the most important tourist sites in the city: the Kyoto Gosho (Imperial Palace) and Nijo Castle, as well a few smaller sights and attractions. This part of town is basically everything that’s in the middle of Kyoto. Central Kyoto can also be subdivided into a few different parts.

Downtown Kyoto

This area of Central Kyoto is loaded with hotels, shops, restaurants, bars, and clubs. It is not a main sightseeing destination, but there are a few good sights scattered about. Downtown Kyoto is a roughly square area, and because it’s not too big, it’s best explored on foot. Also, with lots of covered arcades, it’s one of the best places to spend a rainy day. Downtown Kyoto is the area bounded on the east by the Kamogawa River, on the west by Karasuma-dori, on the north by Oike-dori, and on the south by Shijo-dori.

Kyoto Station Area

This is typically everyone’s main entry point into Kyoto, since the stations serves most of major bullet train routes that serve Kyoto. This area is comprised of the station itself and the surrounding blocks, and the main activities here are shopping in the giant malls and electronics emporiums in and around the station. There are also a few temples within walking distance of the station.

Nishijin

This is Kyoto’s famed weaving district, and is located in the northwest part of Central Kyoto. Nishijin is located in the several blocks north and west of the Imadegawa-Horikawa intersection. There is some nice old-school Japan architecture here, and some museums and galleries devoted to Kyoto’s traditional weaving industry. This is a great part of town to visit for the “Old Kyoto” atmosphere.

Southern Kyoto (Rakunan - 洛南)

Southern Kyoto, at the far southern end of the Higashiyama Mountains, is one Kyoto’s richest areas for sightseeing and covers a large part of the overall Kyoto area. There are tons of temples and shrines in this area, and there are also some areas of splendid nature. There are two major sub-areas of Southern Kyoto.

Southeastern Kyoto

This area is home to two of Kyoto’s greatest sights: The Fushimi-Inari-Taisha Shrine, and the Tofuku-ji Temple and the smaller temples that surround it. Though the Fishimi-Inari Shrine is one of Kyoto’s most visited places, the surrounding nature, parks, gardens, temples, and shrines provide less-crowded spaces for strolling and reflection. Southern Kyoto is the place to enjoy being outside, and to pay your respects to the ancient architecture and gods of the land that are celebrated here.

Oharano Area

The Oharano Area, located in the Southwest, is named after the Oharano Shrine, which is one of the most famous sites in the area. While it is not far from the inner city, it does not reflect any of the stereotypical images one has when they think of Kyoto. The area is so rural, it feels as though the nearest city is hundreds of miles away. Most tourists are not even aware this area exists, so it can be a nice change of pace for those who have the time to explore the outer regions.

Eastern Kyoto (Higashiyama - 東山)

This is the home to some of the most picturesque parts of Kyoto, which lies in the eastern part of the city, across the Kamo River. Our AirBnB is located near this part of the city (we’ll be just across the Kamo River on the western bank). Eastern Kyoto is one of the city’s best preserved historic districts. The streets here are lined with small shops, cafes, and restaurants, all of which retain their traditional design. Eastern Kyoto is also home to the small, famous Gion neighborhood.

Gion - 祇園

This area of town is home to several of Kyoto’s Geisha districts, and is also home to many shops, restaurants, and teahouses where the geisha entertain guests. This area is located to the north-west of the Kiyomizu-dera. Due to recent issues with aggressive tourists in the area trying to touch the geisha and/or take photos of them, there have been recent bans on photo taking in some areas of this part of town, so keep an eye out for the posted signs and act accordingly.

Okazaki

This neighborhood is located in the Northern part of the Higashiyama district, and is known locally as “Museum District” due to all the museums in the area. It also home to the Hiean-jingu Shrine and some really beautiful parks. This area of town is home to wide streets, spacious sidewalks, and plenty of open space.
